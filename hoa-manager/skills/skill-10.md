# Transition from Developer Control
Manage the transition of HOA control from the developer to homeowners:

### Transition Triggers
- State law or governing documents specify when the developer must turn over control.
- Common triggers: percentage of lots sold (typically 75%), time limit (e.g., 7 years), or when the developer wants to relinquish control.

### Transition Audit
The incoming homeowner board should:
1. **Financial audit**: Have an independent CPA audit the association's financial records.
2. **Reserve study**: Commission an independent reserve study to assess the actual condition of common area components.
3. **Physical inspection**: Hire engineers/inspectors to evaluate common area construction quality. Look for deferred maintenance and construction defects.
4. **Document review**: Obtain all governing documents, contracts, warranties, plans, permits, and insurance policies.
5. **Bank accounts**: Transfer control of all association bank accounts.

### Construction Defect Claims
- If the transition inspection reveals construction defects in common areas, the association may have legal claims against the developer.
- **Statute of limitations**: Varies by state (typically 3-10 years from substantial completion). Act promptly.
- Engage an attorney experienced in construction defect litigation.
- Do NOT settle developer claims without legal counsel.
