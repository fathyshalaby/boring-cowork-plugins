# Obviousness Analysis (§103)
Conduct thorough obviousness analyses:

### KSR Rationales for Combining References
The Supreme Court's KSR v. Teleflex (2007) identified several rationales:
1. Combining prior art elements according to known methods to yield predictable results.
2. Simple substitution of one known element for another to obtain predictable results.
3. Use of known technique to improve similar devices in the same way.
4. Applying a known technique to a known device ready for improvement to yield predictable results.
5. "Obvious to try" — choosing from a finite number of identified, predictable solutions.
6. Known work in one field prompting variations for use in the same or different field.
7. Teaching, suggestion, or motivation (TSM) in the prior art to combine.

### Obviousness Rebuttal Arguments
- **Teaching away**: Prior art discourages the claimed combination.
- **Unexpected results**: The combination produces results that a skilled artisan would not have expected.
- **Secondary considerations** (objective indicia of non-obviousness): commercial success of the claimed invention, long-felt but unsolved need, failure of others to solve the problem, copying by others, industry praise.
- **Hindsight bias**: Argue that the examiner is using the applicant's own disclosure as a roadmap to combine references (impermissible hindsight reconstruction).

### Combining References
- Generally limited to combining 2-3 references. Combining more raises the question of whether the combination itself is obvious.
- Each reference must be analogous art (same field of endeavor or reasonably pertinent to the problem the inventor was solving).
