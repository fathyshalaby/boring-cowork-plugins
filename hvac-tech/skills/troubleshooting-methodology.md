# Systematic Troubleshooting Methodology
Apply a structured, methodical approach to diagnosing HVAC problems:

### The Diagnostic Framework
Always follow this sequence when helping a tech troubleshoot:

1. **Verify the complaint**: What exactly is the customer experiencing? "No cooling" could mean the system runs but doesn't cool, or doesn't run at all—two completely different diagnostic paths.
2. **Check the basics first**: Thermostat setting and mode, air filter condition, breaker status, and disconnect switch. 80% of service calls are resolved at this step.
3. **Gather data**: Take readings BEFORE making any changes. Record: supply air temp, return air temp (delta-T should be 14–22°F for cooling), suction pressure, discharge pressure, superheat, subcooling, amperage on compressor and fan motor.
4. **Compare to specs**: Look up the manufacturer's charging chart, which gives target superheat/subcooling based on outdoor ambient and indoor wet-bulb temperature.
5. **Isolate the system**: Electrical problem or mechanical problem? If the compressor contactor pulls in but the compressor doesn't start, it's likely electrical (bad capacitor, open winding, locked rotor). If it runs but pressures are wrong, it's mechanical (low charge, restriction, compressor valve failure).
6. **Fix one thing at a time**: Never make multiple changes simultaneously. Fix one issue, re-measure, then evaluate.
7. **Verify the repair**: After the fix, run the system for 15+ minutes and re-take all readings to confirm they're within spec.

### Common Misdiagnoses to Avoid
- **"Low on refrigerant"** is a symptom, not a diagnosis. Find and fix the leak first.
- **Replacing a capacitor** without checking the motor's amp draw. A failed motor can kill a new cap.
- **Blaming the thermostat** before checking the wiring and control board.
