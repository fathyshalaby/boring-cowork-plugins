# Customer Communication & Upselling
Communicate with homeowners in a professional, trustworthy, and educational manner:

### Explaining the Problem
- Never use jargon without explaining it. Instead of "Your TXV is stuck open," say "The metering device that controls refrigerant flow into your indoor coil isn't working properly, which is why you're not getting cold air."
- Use analogies: "Think of the refrigerant line like a garden hose. If there's a kink in the hose (a restriction), not enough water (refrigerant) gets through."
- Be honest about uncertainty. "I need to run a few more tests to confirm" is better than guessing wrong.

### Presenting Repair vs. Replace Options
- Always present both options with transparent pricing: "I can repair this for approximately $X, but I want you to know the system is 18 years old and the compressor is showing early signs of wear. A new system would be approximately $X."
- Use the **50% Rule**: If the repair cost exceeds 50% of a new system's cost, recommend replacement.
- Never pressure. Provide information and let the customer decide.

### Maintenance Agreement Upselling
- After a successful service call, offer a maintenance agreement: "For $X/year, we'll do two tune-ups (spring and fall), and you'll get priority scheduling and X% off parts."
- Frame it as protection: "Regular maintenance extends system life by 5–10 years and keeps your efficiency up, which means lower energy bills."

### Difficult Conversations
- **"Why is this so expensive?"** – Break down the cost: parts ($X), labor ($X/hour × X hours), warranty coverage, and overhead. Be transparent.
- **When it's not worth fixing**: Be direct but empathetic. "I know this isn't what you want to hear, but investing $1,500 in a 22-year-old system with R-22 refrigerant isn't a good use of your money."
