# Refrigerant Handling & Charging
Handle all refrigerants safely and charge systems accurately:

### Common Residential Refrigerants
| Refrigerant | Type | Status | GWP | Typical Use |
|-------------|------|--------|-----|-------------|
| R-22 | HCFC | **Phased out** (Jan 2020 production ban) | 1810 | Legacy AC/heat pumps |
| R-410A | HFC | Current standard, phase-down beginning | 2088 | Modern AC/heat pumps |
| R-32 | HFC | Emerging (lower GWP) | 675 | Next-gen mini-splits |
| R-454B | HFO blend | AIM Act replacement for R-410A | 466 | Future residential systems |

### Charging Methods
1. **Subcooling Method** (TXV systems, most modern equipment):
   - Measure liquid line pressure and temperature at the service valve.
   - Convert pressure to saturation temperature using a P-T chart.
   - Subcooling = Saturation temp − Actual liquid line temp.
   - Target: Manufacturer spec (typically 8–14°F). Add refrigerant if subcooling is low; recover if high.

2. **Superheat Method** (Fixed orifice/piston systems):
   - Measure suction line pressure and temperature at the service valve.
   - Superheat = Actual suction line temp − Saturation temp at suction pressure.
   - Target: Use the manufacturer's charging chart (based on outdoor ambient and indoor wet-bulb).

### Critical Rules
- **Never mix refrigerants.** If unsure what's in the system, recover it all and start fresh.
- **Weigh the charge**: For new installations or after a full recovery, weigh in the exact nameplate charge with a refrigerant scale.
- **Never liquid-charge into the suction line** (this can slug the compressor). Charge as vapor into the low side, or liquid into the high side with the system off.
- **Temperature-pressure relationship**: Always verify with a P-T chart. Pocket apps (e.g., HVAC Check & Charge, Danfoss Ref Tools) are acceptable.
