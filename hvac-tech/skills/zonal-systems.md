# Zoned Systems & Mini-Splits
Advise on zoned HVAC systems and ductless mini-split installations:

### Ducted Zoning Systems
- **How it works**: Motorized dampers in the ductwork open/close based on zone thermostat calls. A zone control panel manages which zones get airflow.
- **Bypass damper or modulating equipment**: When only one zone calls, the system must handle the excess air. Solutions: bypass damper (dumps air back to return), or variable-speed equipment that reduces airflow automatically.
- **Common problems**: Buzzing ductwork (too much static when zones close), short-cycling (insufficient load on a single zone), and failed damper actuators.
- **Best practice**: Variable-speed inverter systems are the best pairing with zoning—they modulate capacity to match partial zone loads.

### Ductless Mini-Split Systems
- **Applications**: Room additions, garage conversions, server rooms, homes without existing ductwork.
- **Sizing**: Size each head individually based on the room's Manual J load. Do NOT oversize—inverter mini-splits perform best at partial load.
- **Installation critical points**:
  - Lineset length: Follow manufacturer max (typically 50–75 feet). Every foot of excess reduces performance.
  - Flare connections: Use a torque wrench to manufacturer specs (not hand-tight). Bad flares are the #1 cause of refrigerant leaks in mini-splits.
  - Condensate drain: Maintaining gravity slope or install a mini condensate pump.
  - Electrical: Dedicated circuit per outdoor unit. Follow NEC for wire gauge and breaker size.
- **Multi-zone**: One outdoor condenser can serve 2–5 indoor heads. Beware of "connected capacity ratio"—most manufacturers allow 100–130% indoor capacity relative to outdoor. Going over causes performance loss.

### When to Recommend Zoning vs. Mini-Splits
- Existing ductwork in good condition → Ducted zoning.
- No existing ductwork or specific problem rooms → Mini-splits.
- Whole-home comfort with room-by-room control + no ducts → Multi-zone mini-split system.
