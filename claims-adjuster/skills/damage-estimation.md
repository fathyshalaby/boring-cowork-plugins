# Damage Assessment & Estimation
Accurately assess and estimate damage for fair claim settlements:

### Property Damage Estimation
- **Replacement Cost Value (RCV)**: Cost to repair or replace with like kind and quality, without deduction for depreciation.
- **Actual Cash Value (ACV)**: RCV minus depreciation. ACV = RCV × (1 - depreciation%). Most policies pay ACV first, then recoverable depreciation upon completion of repairs.
- **Depreciation**: Based on Useful Life Factor (ULF) tables. Example: Asphalt shingle roof with 25-year life, 15 years old = 60% depreciated.
- **Xactimate**: Industry-standard estimating software. Know how to read and write Xactimate estimates. Practice line-item breakdown.
- **Scope the damage**: Walk the entire property systematically. Don't just look at what the claimant shows you—check everywhere.

### Auto Damage Estimation
- **Repairable vs. Total Loss**: If repair cost exceeds the vehicle's ACV (typically at 70-75% threshold), the vehicle is a total loss.
- **Total loss valuation**: Use CCC, Mitchell, or Audatex valuation reports. Compare to local market listings of comparable vehicles.
- **Diminished value**: Some jurisdictions allow the claimant to recover the loss in market value after repair, even if the repair is complete.
- **Supplemental claims**: Initial estimates may miss hidden damage. Honor legitimate supplements from repair shops.

### Bodily Injury Evaluation
- **Medical specials**: Total medical bills (past and estimated future treatment).
- **General damages**: Pain and suffering, emotional distress, loss of enjoyment of life. Typically evaluated as a multiple of specials (varies by jurisdiction and severity).
- **Lost wages**: Past and future lost earnings due to the injury.
- **Permanency**: If the injury results in permanent impairment, the value increases significantly.
- **Pre-existing conditions**: "Eggshell plaintiff" rule—you take the claimant as you find them. A pre-existing condition aggravated by the incident is compensable.
