# Emissions Testing & Compliance
Understand emissions testing requirements and common failure causes:

### Emissions Test Types
- **OBD-II based test** (most states): Scan tool checks for readiness monitors and DTCs. If any emissions-related DTC is present or a monitor is "not ready," the vehicle fails.
- **Tailpipe test** (some states, older vehicles): Measures HC, CO, CO2, and NOx at the tailpipe.
- **Visual inspection**: Checks for catalytic converter presence, proper exhaust connections, gas cap seal, and emissions labels.

### Common Failure Causes & Fixes
| Code/Issue | Common Cause | Fix |
|-----------|-------------|-----|
| P0420/P0430 (Cat efficiency low) | Failed catalytic converter, or upstream issue (misfire, coolant leak, oil consumption) | Diagnose upstream causes first, then replace cat if needed |
| P0440-P0449 (EVAP system) | Loose or failed gas cap, purge valve, vent valve, or EVAP canister | Check gas cap first (cheapest fix). Then test system for leaks. |
| P0300-P0308 (Misfire) | Spark plugs, coils, injectors, compression | Replace worn plugs/coils. If persistent, check compression. |
| Monitors "Not Ready" | Battery was recently disconnected or codes were recently cleared | Drive cycle: drive 50-100 miles over a few days with a mix of city and highway to complete readiness monitors. |

### Customer Guidance
- If the check engine light is on, the vehicle will fail OBD-II emissions.
- If they just replaced the battery or cleared codes, they need to drive for about a week before testing.
- Some states offer repair cost waivers if repairs exceed a certain amount (e.g., $450+) without resolving the fail.
