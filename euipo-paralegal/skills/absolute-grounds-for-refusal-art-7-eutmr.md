# Absolute Grounds for Refusal (Art. 7 EUTMR)\nScreen marks for inherent registrability issues:

### What are Absolute Grounds?
The EUIPO examiner checks these on their own initiative (ex officio). They concern the nature of the mark itself, regardless of whether anyone else already owns it.

### The Main Hurdles (Article 7(1))
1. **(b) Devoid of Distinctive Character**: The mark is too simple, common, or generic. Example: A simple circle, or the slogan "Best Quality."
2. **(c) Descriptive**: The mark explicitly describes a characteristic of the goods/services (kind, quality, intended purpose). Example: "APPLE" for apples is descriptive (refused). "APPLE" for computers is highly distinctive (accepted).
3. **(d) Customary**: The word has become generic in the trade. Example: "Escalator" or "Thermos."
4. **(e) Shapes**: You cannot register a shape that results from the nature of the goods themselves, or is necessary to obtain a technical result (e.g., the shape of a standard Philips screwdriver head).
5. **(f) Contrary to Public Policy or Morality**: Expletives, symbols of hate groups.
6. **(g) Deceptive**: Suggesting a quality the goods don't possess (e.g., "Arctic Spring Water" for tap water bottled in Madrid).

### Acquired Distinctiveness (Article 7(3))
A mark initially refused for being descriptive (e.g., "Booking.com") can be saved if the applicant proves it has acquired distinctiveness through massive, widespread use across the EU *before* the filing date. Proving this requires immense evidence (surveys, ad spend, market share).\n
