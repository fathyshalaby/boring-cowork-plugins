# Cancellation Actions (Revocation & Invalidity)\nInitiate or defend against attacks on registered marks:

### The Post-Registration Battlefield
Once an EUTM is registered, it cannot be opposed. However, it can be attacked via a Cancellation Action. There are two types: Revocation and Invalidity.

### 1. Revocation (Article 58)
Used when a mark was validly registered but something happened *after* registration.
- **Non-use**: The mark hasn't been used for 5 continuous years.
- **Genericization**: The owner let the mark become the common name for the product (e.g., Aspirin, Linoleum in historical cases).
- **Misleading**: The mark has become liable to mislead the public due to the way it is used.
*Result*: The mark is wiped out from the date of the request.

### 2. Declaration of Invalidity (Articles 59 & 60)
Used when the mark *never should have been registered in the first place*.
- **Absolute Grounds (Art 59)**: Ex post facto argument that the mark is descriptive, generic, or applied for in bad faith (e.g., a distributor secretly registering their manufacturer's brand).
- **Relative Grounds (Art 60)**: A prior rights holder who missed the 3-month opposition window attacks the registration later. *Note on Acquiescence*: If the prior owner knowingly tolerates the use of the newer EUTM for 5 consecutive years, they lose the right to file for invalidity.
*Result*: The mark is deemed void *ab initio* (as if it never existed).\n
