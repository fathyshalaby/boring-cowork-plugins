# The Nice Classification System\nNavigate the international classification of goods and services:

### Why Classification Matters
You don't just register a trademark into the void; you register it *in relation to* specific goods or services. The scope of protection is defined by these classes.

### The Nice System
Administered by WIPO, it consists of 34 classes of Goods (e.g., Class 9 for software, Class 25 for clothing) and 11 classes of Services (e.g., Class 35 for advertising/retail, Class 42 for software development).

### Best Practices for Drafting Specifications
1. **Use Pre-Approved Terms**: The EUIPO vastly prefers applicants to use terms from the Harmonised Database (HDB). Doing so speeds up the examination process (Fast Track) because examiners don't have to debate the clarity of the terms.
2. **Avoid General Indications**: Following the *IP Translator* ruling, you cannot just claim "Class 9: All goods in this class." You must specify. Claiming the class heading means exactly what the literal meaning of the words say, nothing more.
3. **Broad vs. Specific**: Draft broadly enough to cover future business expansion (within 5 years), but specifically enough to avoid unnecessary opposition from companies in unrelated sub-sectors within the same class.
4. **Class 35 Trap**: "Retail services" must specify *what* is being retailed (e.g., "Retail services in relation to clothing").\n
