# Cleaning Chemical Knowledge & Safety
Understand cleaning chemicals, their proper use, and safety requirements:

### Chemical Categories
| Category | Examples | Use | Safety |
|----------|---------|-----|--------|
| Neutral pH Cleaners (pH 6-8) | General purpose cleaners | Daily cleaning of most surfaces | Lowest risk, safe on most finishes |
| Alkaline Cleaners (pH 9-13) | Degreasers, strippers, oven cleaners | Grease/soil removal, floor stripping | Can damage aluminum, skin/eye irritant |
| Acid Cleaners (pH 1-5) | Bathroom cleaners, lime removers | Mineral deposits, rust, scale | Corrosive, never mix with bleach |
| Disinfectants | Quats, bleach, hydrogen peroxide | Kill bacteria/viruses on surfaces | Follow dwell time requirements |
| Sanitizers | Food-contact surface sanitizers | Reduce bacteria to safe levels | Must be food-safe for food areas |

### Critical Safety Rules
- **NEVER mix bleach (sodium hypochlorite) with ammonia-based cleaners.** This creates chloramine gas, which is toxic and potentially lethal.
- **NEVER mix bleach with acid cleaners.** This creates chlorine gas.
- All chemicals must have Safety Data Sheets (SDS) accessible within the facility.
- Proper PPE per the SDS: at minimum, gloves and eye protection for most chemicals. Respirator for stripping operations.
- Dilution ratios matter. Too concentrated = surface damage, chemical waste, safety risk. Too diluted = ineffective.

### Disinfection vs. Cleaning
- **Cleaning** removes dirt, debris, and some germs. It visibly improves appearance.
- **Disinfecting** kills germs on surfaces. Requires EPA-registered disinfectant, proper dilution, and adequate **dwell time** (contact time on the surface, typically 1-10 minutes).
- Always **clean before disinfecting**. Disinfectants don't work well on dirty surfaces.
