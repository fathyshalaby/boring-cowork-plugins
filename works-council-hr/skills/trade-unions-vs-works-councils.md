# Trade Unions vs. Works Councils
Differentiate between trade union rights and Works Council rights:

### The Dual Channel System (e.g., Germany, France)
- **Works Councils**: Elected by ALL eligible employees in a company/establishment, regardless of union membership. Primarily focused on co-determination, social matters, day-to-day operations, and protecting employee rights at the company level. Bound by an obligation of "trustful cooperation" and "peace obligation" (cannot call strikes).
- **Trade Unions**: Voluntary membership organizations. Primarily focused on collective bargaining for wages, salary grids, industry-wide working hours, and broad employment conditions. They hold the legal right to organize industrial action (strikes).

### The Single Channel System (e.g., UK, Nordics)
- Workplace representation is primarily channeled through recognized trade unions (Shop Stewards). Works Councils exist mostly as European obligations (EWC) or limited information/consultation forums.

### Interaction in Practice
- In Germany: The Works Council cannot negotiate base salaries or collective working times if these are regulated by a collective bargaining agreement (Tarifvertrag) negotiated by the union—unless the union agreement specifically permits local deviation (an "opening clause").
- In France: Unions (délégués syndicaux) have a monopoly on negotiating collective agreements (accord d'entreprise), while the CSE handles information/consultation. If no union delegates exist, the CSE may negotiate under certain strict conditions.
