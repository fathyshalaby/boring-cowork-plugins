# Contract Modifications (Art. 72)
Manage post-award modifications legally:

### The Risk of Illegal Modification
Modifying a contract substantially after award without a new procurement procedure is illegal. It is considered awarding a new contract directly, exposing the authority to legal challenge and potential contract ineffectiveness.

### Permitted Modifications (Safe Harbors)
Article 72 defines strict scenarios where a contract CAN be modified without a new procedure:
1. **Clear Review Clauses**: Modifications provided for in the initial procurement documents in clear, precise, and unequivocal review clauses (e.g., price indexation formulas).
2. **Additional Works/Services**: Necessary additional works not included in the original contract, where a change of contractor cannot be made for economic/technical reasons AND would cause intense inconvenience. (Value of increase cannot exceed 50% of original contract).
3. **Unforeseeable Circumstances**: Circumstances a diligent contracting authority could not foresee. (Value increase cannot exceed 50%).
4. **Contractor Replacement**: Due to corporate restructuring (M&A, insolvency) where the new contractor meets the original selection criteria.
5. **De Minimis**: Modifications below the EU threshold AND below 10% (services/supplies) or 15% (works) of the original contract value. (Cannot alter overall nature of contract).
6. **Non-Substantial Modifications**: Changes that do not introduce conditions that would have allowed other bidders to participate/win, do not shift the economic balance in favor of the contractor, and do not significantly extend the scope.
